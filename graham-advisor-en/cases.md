# Benjamin Graham Case Studies

## Case 1: Graham Buys GEICO — Classic Contrarian Investment

**Background:** In the 1950s, Buffett (Graham's student) discovered GEICO. Graham bought shares at around $1 each, far below the company's working capital.

**Dilemma:** GEICO was losing money due to insurance business expansion. The market thought the company had problems.

**First Principle Judgment:** The company's liquidation value far exceeded its stock price. An insurance company's float (premiums held before claims) is itself a valuable asset.

**Key Action:** Graham bought when the price was far below intrinsic value, creating extremely high margin of safety.

**Result:** GEICO became one of Buffett's most successful investments. Berkshire Hathaway eventually acquired GEICO.

**Framework Elements Demonstrated:**
- Margin of safety principle: price far below liquidation value
- Contrarian thinking: buying when market panics
- Quantitative analysis: valuation using liquidation value method

**Transferable Insight:** When a company's cash assets exceed what you pay for the stock, it usually means exceptional margin of safety.

## Case 2: 1973-74 Crash Operations — Finding Opportunity in Panic

**Background:** 1973-74 was one of the worst post-war bear markets in the US, with inflation soaring and stocks plummeting.

**Dilemma:** Investors were panicking, dumping stocks indiscriminately.

**First Principle Judgment:** Panicking markets often create opportunities with extremely high margin of safety.

**Key Action:** Graham looked for quality companies wrongly sold off during this period, filtering by his criteria: low P/E, low price-to-book ratio, strong financial position.

**Result:** He built valuable positions during this period that paid off well.

**Framework Elements Demonstrated:**
- Mr. Market allegory: panic markets provide better buying opportunities
- Contrarian investing: being greedy when others are fearful
- Patience: not rushing to act, waiting for the perfect price

**Transferable Insight:** Bear markets are value investors' best friends—provided you have cash and courage.

## Case 3: Long-term Hold of Washington Post — Patience for Value Return

**Background:** In the early 1970s, Washington Post fell about 50% from its high due to Watergate and overall market decline.

**Dilemma:** Stock price plummeted, market confidence collapsed.

**First Principle Judgment:** The media business's franchise and brand value hadn't been impaired. The price drop created opportunity, not risk.

**Key Action:** Buffett (influenced by Graham) bought Washington Post at far below intrinsic value.

**Result:** This investment returned tens of times.

**Framework Elements Demonstrated:**
- Distinguishing value from price: business quality and stock price fluctuation are different things
- Long-term holding: patience not to sell due to short-term volatility
- Circle of competence: understanding the media industry's competitive advantages

**Transferable Insight:** When good companies' stock prices crash due to short-term events, it's often the best buying opportunity.

## Case 4: The Master-Student Relationship with Buffett — Legacy and Evolution

**Background:** In the 1950s, Buffett studied Graham's course at Columbia University and later worked at Graham's firm.

**Dilemma:** Graham's methodology was "cigarette butt investing"—buying mediocre companies at extremely low prices, pursuing liquidation value.

**First Principle Judgment:** Buffett realized Graham's approach had limitations and began exploring buying good companies at reasonable prices.

**Key Action:** Buffett didn't completely copy Graham's method. Instead, he evolved Graham's framework, eventually forming his own investment philosophy.

**Result:** Buffett combined Graham's "margin of safety" with Fisher's "growth stock" concepts, becoming one of history's most successful investors.

**Framework Elements Demonstrated:**
- Margin of safety is core: never violate this principle
- Methodology can evolve: not dogmatic
- Independent thinking: can surpass even your teacher's theories

**Transferable Insight:** Learning a framework doesn't mean copying it 100%. Master its core and evolve it in practice.
